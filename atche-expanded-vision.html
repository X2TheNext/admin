What Came Out of the Room
The Four Ideas
These aren't random features. They're four interconnected layers that — built in the right order — turn ATCHÉ from a community app into something venues and artists can't function without.
⭐
Michelin-Style Venue Certification
Venues are rated against a set of ATCHÉ-defined standards. To carry the ATCHÉ certification badge publicly, they must meet and maintain the score. Xavier's add: sell that standard back to them as a paid product.
💰
Revenue Attribution
When an ATCHÉ artist performs at a venue, ATCHÉ tracks how much revenue is generated that night. Proves the artist's economic value — to the venue, to the artist, and to the booking market.
📲
Native Artist Booking
Venues book ATCHÉ artists directly through the app. ATCHÉ is the transaction layer, not just the discovery layer. Booking fee revenue, automated contracts, and built-in protections.
✍🏾
Open Artist Commentary
Artists leave public reviews and comments about venues — payment practices, environment, professionalism. Community-validated scoring, not just top-down certification. Glassdoor for nightlife.
The Real Insight
These Four Are One System
Standalone, each idea is useful. Together, they form a closed loop that gets more powerful the more people use it.
The Flywheel
Artists comment on venues → their commentary feeds the rating score → venues want a better score → they pay for certification and improve their standards → ATCHÉ books more artists into certified venues → revenue attribution proves artists drive real dollars → venues pay more to book ATCHÉ talent → more artists join ATCHÉ to access those bookings → repeat.
Layer 1
Commentary is the foundation
Artist reviews are the raw data the entire system runs on. Without honest, community-sourced input on how venues actually treat performers, the rating is just ATCHÉ's opinion. With it, the rating has credibility that's very hard to challenge or fake. This is also a network effect — every artist who joins and comments makes the data better for every other artist.
Layer 2
Certification creates the stakes
The commentary feeds a score. The score feeds a certification tier. Once ATCHÉ's "Certified" badge means something in DC nightlife — once people know that only venues who actually pay artists on time and maintain a certain environment can carry it — non-certified venues feel the gap. That's when you can charge for the badge. And when you can set the criteria, you set the standard for the industry.
Layer 3
Revenue attribution is the proof
This is what makes the booking fee unchallengeable. Right now, venues don't know if a specific artist drove revenue — they feel it, but they can't prove it. If ATCHÉ can show a venue that revenue went up 40% on the nights [Name] performed, the artist can charge more, ATCHÉ's cut is justified, and the booking fee becomes an ROI conversation instead of a cost conversation. This data also protects artists — it's evidence for fair pay disputes.
Layer 4
Booking is where the money moves
Once trust is established through certification and attribution, venues naturally want to book ATCHÉ talent through ATCHÉ — because the booking comes with data, contracts, protections, and the ATCHÉ brand behind it. That's where the transaction fee revenue compounds. Each booking is also another data point for the attribution model.

How It Scales
Three Phases
The critical move is building the rating system quietly before making it public. Once it goes live, it has weight. The AV expert was right about that.
1
Phase 1 · DC · Now – 12 months
Build the Standard Quietly
Pilot with Haven and 2–3 other warm DC venues. Don't publish scores yet.
Define the ATCHÉ certification criteria — payment terms, women's safety protocols, % female performers booked, environment standards. Get input from Circle members.
Build artist commentary into the app (private beta first — Circle members only).
Start tracking check-ins and attendance at pilot venues to build the attribution data foundation.
Haven becomes the first "ATCHÉ Certified" venue — a proof point, not a product yet.
2
Phase 2 · DMV + Atlanta · 12–24 months
Make It Public, Make It a Product
Launch the ATCHÉ Venue Rating publicly. First 5–10 venues rated. The certification badge goes live.
Non-certified venues see the gap. Some will reach out. That's when you charge.
Launch native booking in the app — start in DC, expand to Atlanta.
Artist commentary goes public. Venues can respond to reviews (like Glassdoor employer responses).
First attribution reports sold to venues as premium add-ons.
3
Phase 3 · National · 24–48 months
The ATCHÉ Standard Is the Industry Standard
"ATCHÉ Certified" has national brand recognition in nightlife. Venues in every market want to carry it — because their target audience (creative women, curated guests) use the app to decide where to go.
Attribution data spans multiple cities. You can benchmark DC vs Atlanta vs Charlotte — sell that data to brands and venue groups.
The booking marketplace becomes a national platform. Artists can get booked in cities they've never been to, with ATCHÉ handling contracts and payments.
At this scale, the certification + data business alone could support a Series A fundraise.
Why This Is Hard to Copy
Three Moats
01
Community trust moat
Artist commentary is only credible if artists actually trust the platform enough to be honest. ATCHÉ's Circle-first, women-protective positioning is why they will. A competitor can copy the feature, not the trust.
02
Data moat
Revenue attribution requires historical data. The longer ATCHÉ tracks check-ins, artist performances, and venue revenue, the more accurate and valuable the attribution model gets. Day 1 competitors start with zero data.
03
Standard-setting moat
Whoever defines the criteria for what makes a "good" nightlife venue for women becomes the authority. Once ATCHÉ sets that standard publicly and venues compete to meet it, the standard is ATCHÉ's. That's very hard to dislodge.

Lock in Haven as Pilot Partner
Follow up with the AV expert while the meeting energy is fresh. The ask isn't money — it's permission to use Haven as the first pilot venue for the ATCHÉ certification. Get a date on the calendar. This gives you a real venue to build around and a well-connected advocate in your corner.

Write the first version of the ATCHÉ Venue Standard — what does a venue have to do to earn the badge? Start with the obvious categories: payment terms (do they pay on time? in full?), safety protocols, percentage of female performers booked, atmosphere for women. Get input from Circle members and from the AV expert. This doc doesn't have to be public yet — it just has to exist so the pilot is real.
Next 60 days
Build Artist Commentary (Private)
Add a private "venue review" feature to the app — Circle members only, not public yet. Start collecting honest data about how venues treat performers. Even 20–30 data points from real experiences makes your eventual public rating credible. When you launch publicly, you're not starting from zero.
Don't build yet
Revenue Attribution & Native Booking
These are real and important, but they require infrastructure (payment processing, check-in data, probably a POS integration or QR check-in system) that you don't need to build right now. File them as Phase 2. Focus Phase 1 on community + certification. The data and booking layers are stronger once there's a community to back them.
